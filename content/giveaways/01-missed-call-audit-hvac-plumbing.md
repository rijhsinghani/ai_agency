# The missed call problem: what it's costing HVAC owners and plumbers

A self-audit for solo operators and small crews in the trades

---

## The math you are not tracking

Most plumbers and HVAC techs know they are losing leads. Very few know the number. There is no missed call report in your voicemail app, no log of the callers who hung up before the beep. You are working with a gut feeling — and gut feelings almost always undercount the real damage.

Here is the math using conservative numbers. The average HVAC service call or plumbing job runs $400–$700. A repair or install is $1,000–$3,000+. For this exercise, use $600 as a conservative baseline.

If you miss 5 calls per week, and 40% of those are genuine leads (conservative), that is 2 real leads per week — 8 per month. If 25% of those convert, that is 2 jobs per month. At $600 per job, that is $1,200 per month walking out the door.

Most operators I talk to have no idea how much this is. They just know something feels off.

This comment from r/HVAC says it plainly — 445 upvotes means this person was not alone: "Missed 3 calls today while on a roof — I know I lost at least 2 jobs to a competitor."

The number is probably higher than the estimate above. The math only counts the calls you know you missed — not the callers who hung up before voicemail, scrolled down the list, and called your competitor instead.

## Why calling back does not fix it

The standard advice is to call people back. You already know this does not work consistently. Here is why.

The 2-hour window is the core problem. Industry patterns show that leads who do not hear back within 2 hours are already talking to someone else. That is not a long time when you are under a crawl space or halfway through a system install.

Even when you remember to call back, call-back fatigue kills the follow-through. You finish a 6-hour job physically drained. Calling 4 strangers back on the drive home sounds reasonable in principle. In practice, it does not happen every time — and "not every time" is enough to lose leads.

Most missed callers do not leave voicemails, which means you do not even have a number to call back. The lead evaporates completely, and you never knew they called.

The receptionist option sounds like a solution. A part-time receptionist runs $1,500–$2,500 per month, and she still cannot take calls when you are working nights, early mornings, or weekends. You would be paying for partial coverage and still missing calls.

The problem is not that you do not care about leads. It is that you are physically occupied at exactly the moment they call.

## How the automated version works

The moment a call goes unanswered, a text fires to the caller within 30 seconds. Not a minute. Thirty seconds — before they scroll to the next name on their list.

The message reads like you wrote it: "Hey, caught your call — I'm on a job right now. Can you tell me what you need and the best time to reach you? I'll get back to you as soon as I wrap up."

Most people reply. The lead stays warm.

When they reply, you get notified. You take the conversation from there — no app to open, no extra step. The system logs every missed call: caller number, time of call, whether they replied, and current status. You always know where every lead stands.

The trigger sounds simple: missed call, send text. The build is not. Your business phone system has to hand off the "missed call" event to a workflow engine, which fires the SMS through a provider like Twilio, stores the lead in a log, and monitors the thread for a reply. The integration depends on your carrier, your phone system type (VoIP vs. landline vs. forwarding), and your CRM if you use one. That configuration varies — and it is exactly where people get stuck when they try this on their own.
