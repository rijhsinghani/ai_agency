Structure the document with 5 sections. Do NOT use the word "section" as a label. Each section title is a sentence-case plain-English heading (no colon, no dash). Section titles should name the reader's reality, not introduce a topic.

**Section 1: The math you are not tracking**

Open with: a framing statement that most trade owners know they are losing leads but do not know the number. Then walk through the math:

- Establish that the average trade job (HVAC service call or plumbing job) is worth $400–$700 for a service call, $1,000–$3,000+ for a repair or install. Use $600 as a conservative baseline throughout.
- Estimate: if a solo operator misses 5 calls per week, and 40% are genuine leads (conservative), that is 2 real leads per week — 8 per month.
- If 25% of those convert (conservative close rate), that is 2 jobs per month = $1,200/month minimum.
- State: "Most operators I talk to have no idea how much this is. They just know something feels off."
- Use the Reddit quote from the HVAC owner (445 upvotes, r/HVAC) — attribute it clearly as a real community quote, not a client.
- End with a friction sentence: the number is probably higher than the estimate because the math only counts the calls you know you missed, not the ones where the caller hung up before voicemail.

**Section 2: Why calling back does not fix it**

Explain why the manual callback approach fails:

- The 2-hour window: industry pattern — leads who do not hear back within 2 hours are already talking to someone else.
- Call-back fatigue: you are mentally occupied after a 6-hour job. Calling 4 strangers back on the drive home is not consistent behavior.
- Voicemail black hole: most people do not leave voicemails, so you do not even have a number to call back.
- Receptionist cost vs. benefit: a part-time receptionist to cover calls is $1,500–$2,500/month and still misses after-hours calls.
- Pair each point with a friction note showing why the human-manual version breaks under real trade-business conditions.

End with: "The problem is not that you do not care about leads. It is that you are physically occupied at exactly the moment they call."

**Section 3: How the automated version works**

Explain the system in plain language — enough to understand it, not enough to build it:

- When a call goes unanswered, a text fires within 30 seconds. Not a minute. Thirty seconds — before they scroll to the next name.
- The message reads like you wrote it (give the example from the capability brief: "Hey, caught your call — I'm on a job right now. Can you tell me what you need and the best time to reach you?").
- Most people reply. The lead stays warm.
- When they reply, you get notified. You take the conversation from there — no app, no extra step.
- Every missed call is logged: caller number, time, whether they replied. You always know where every lead stands.

Then add the friction paragraph — this is the buyer-qualifying paragraph, not optional:

"The trigger sounds simple: missed call → send text. The build is not. Your business phone system has to hand off the 'missed call' event to a workflow engine, which fires the SMS through a provider like Twilio, stores the lead in a log, and monitors the thread for a reply. The integration depends on your carrier, your phone system type (VoIP vs. landline vs. forwarding), and your CRM if you use one. That configuration varies — and it is exactly where people get stuck when they try this on their own."

This paragraph informs without enabling. The reader understands the value, not the build steps.

Below the table, add 2 sentences: Build cost is $1,500 (pays back in under 2 recovered jobs at $600 average). Monthly retainer after that is $200 — less than a single lost job.

**Section 5: Is this right for your business?**

This section delivers the "audit" in the title. It must give the reader a concrete output — a self-assessment they can complete in 60 seconds.

Intro (1 sentence): "Answer these questions honestly."

Checklist (numbered, yes/no format):

1. Do you miss 5 or more calls per week while on a job?
2. Is your average job worth $500 or more?
3. Do you have no way to know how many missed callers went to a competitor?
4. Has a customer ever told you they called but did not leave a voicemail?
5. Have you tried calling back and found the lead had already booked someone else?

Scoring: "If you answered yes to 3 or more: you have a missed-call problem that is costing you real money right now."
