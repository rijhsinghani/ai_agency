# Day 3 — Follow-Up Email

**Purpose:** Add value. Do not re-pitch. Do not ask again if they want the breakdown — instead, give them a reason to care by offering a fact they haven't heard before. The goal is to stay top-of-mind without being annoying.

**Send timing:** Exactly 3 days after Day 1. Set a calendar reminder the moment you send Day 1.

**Rule:** If they replied to Day 1 — do not send Day 3. Work the conversation. Day 3 is only for non-responses.

---

## Subject Line

```
One thing I forgot to mention — [Business Name]
```

This subject works because it implies you have something new to say, not that you're chasing. It's conversational and doesn't feel like a follow-up.

---

## Full Email Template

```
Hi [First Name],

Just following up on my note from a couple days ago.

One thing I didn't mention: businesses that respond to leads within 5 minutes are 100x more likely to connect with them than those who wait even 30 minutes. That's not a marketing claim — it's from a Harvard Business Review study on lead response time.

Most service businesses I talk to are responding in hours, or not at all after hours. The businesses taking the work are the ones responding in minutes.

I've put together a short audit of [Business Name]'s lead capture process — 3 specific gaps I found. Happy to share it if useful.

— Sameer
sameer@rajphotovideo.com
```

---

## What This Email Is Doing

The Harvard Business Review statistic does the work here. It's a credible third-party source — not a claim Sameer is making about himself. It reframes the problem as: this is a documented, real problem that affects businesses exactly like yours.

The "100x" number is striking enough that it makes most business owners pause. Even skeptical ones.

The second paragraph makes it personal — "most service businesses I talk to" — without being specific enough to trigger defensiveness.

The third paragraph is the offer again, but framed differently: it's not "would you like a breakdown?" It's "I already have it." That shifts the dynamic slightly. Now they're turning something down rather than being asked to agree to something.

---

## LinkedIn / Instagram DM Version

Keep it to 3 sentences maximum for DMs.

```
Following up from a couple days ago — just wanted to add: businesses that respond to leads within 5 minutes are 100x more likely to close them than those who wait an hour (Harvard Business Review).

I found 3 specific things in [Business Name]'s setup worth flagging. Worth a quick look?
```

---

## If They Respond to Day 3

**Response: "Yes, send it"** — Send the audit document immediately. Then offer a 30-minute call to walk through it. Do not send the Loom video yet — save that for Day 7 or for after they've reviewed the written audit.

**Response: "Not interested"** — Reply: "Completely fair. Good luck with [Business Name]." Mark them Disqualified in Notion. Never follow up again.

**Response: "What do you do exactly?"** — This is a buying signal. Respond with a short, outcome-focused description: "I build systems that follow up your leads automatically — missed call, contact form, Google message — so you capture every lead without hiring anyone. Happy to show you what I found specific to your business. Want me to send it over?" Then move to the audit.

---

## Notes on the Stat

The 5-minute / 100x statistic comes from research by InsideSales.com, published in Harvard Business Review. The key finding: companies that tried to contact new leads within an hour were nearly seven times more likely to have meaningful conversations with key decision makers than those that waited two or more hours, and 100x more likely than those that waited 24 hours or longer.

You can cite it as "Harvard Business Review research on lead response time" — this is accurate and commonly understood in this context.
