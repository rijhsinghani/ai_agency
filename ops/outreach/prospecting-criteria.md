# Prospecting Criteria

**Purpose:** Define exactly which businesses to pursue, how to score them, and how to track everything. Quality over quantity — a list of 50 well-researched businesses outperforms a list of 500 names.

**Geography:** Start hyper-local. Pick one metro area or city neighborhood. Local means you can show up in person if needed, and it gives you credibility in outreach: "I work with businesses in [City]."

## Target Business Types — Priority Order

| Priority | Business Type                  | Why They're a Target                                                                                     | Key Pain Signal                                               | Estimated Job Value     |
| -------- | ------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| 1        | Plumbers                       | Miss calls constantly when on jobsites. Can't answer phones when their hands are in a drain.             | Reviews saying "hard to reach" or "didn't call back"          | $800–$2,000 per job     |
| 2        | HVAC Contractors               | Seasonal surges — peak summer and winter — create call overflow they can't handle.                       | Slow after-hours response, long hold times during busy season | $500–$3,000 per job     |
| 3        | Roofers / General Contractors  | Storm season creates enormous lead spikes. Estimates go unsent. Leads go cold.                           | Delayed estimate follow-up, no follow-up at all               | $5,000–$15,000 per job  |
| 4        | Dental / Orthodontic Practices | Appointment-heavy operations. No-shows and cancellations cost real money. Reminders pay off immediately. | High no-show rate, manual reminder process                    | $200–$800 per visit     |
| 5        | Realtors / Real Estate Teams   | Speed-to-lead is documented to be the single biggest factor in closing.                                  | "Didn't hear back quickly" in reviews                         | $5,000–$15,000 per deal |
| 6        | Home Cleaning Services         | Recurring scheduling is a manual grind. Review requests after each visit are high-impact.                | Manual rebooking, no automated reminders                      | $150–$300 per clean     |

**Start with Priority 1 and 2.** Plumbers and HVAC are high-contrast enough to test different messaging, but similar enough in pain profile that the same core audit framework works for both. Once you have a case study from one of these, the other business types become easier to approach.

## 3-Point Qualification Scoring System

A prospect must meet at least 2 of these 3 criteria to make the outreach list. Score each prospect before adding them.

### Criterion 1: Revenue Signal (They Are Doing Real Volume)

They show signs of being an active, operating business — not a part-time side operation. Look for:

- 4 or more Google reviews
- In business 3 or more years (check their Google Business Profile for "Opened" date or their website footer)
- Actual website (not just a Facebook page or a single Google Maps listing)
- Multiple employees or crews visible on their website or in photos

**Scores 1 point if:** Any 2 of the above are present.

---

### Criterion 2: Pain Signal (Evidence of the Problem We Solve)

There is visible, public evidence that they struggle with lead response, communication, or follow-up. Look for:

- A Google, Yelp, or Facebook review that explicitly mentions slow response, hard to reach, didn't call back, or similar
- A contact form with a response time that, when tested, exceeds 1 hour
- A Google Business Profile with no messaging enabled and no alternative to calling
- A website with "call us" as the only contact method and no after-hours option

**Scores 1 point if:** At least one of the above is clearly present.

**Important:** This is the criterion that makes outreach personal. If you find a specific review quoting "I left three voicemails and never heard back," that quote goes in the Day 1 email. That specificity is what gets opens and replies.

---

### Criterion 3: Non-Technical Owner (They Don't Already Have Internal Help)

The owner is running the business and making decisions — they don't have a CTO, internal developer, or dedicated marketing team who has already solved this problem. Look for:

- Small team (fewer than 20 employees by any visible signal)
- No mention of "tech team" or "IT" on their website
- Owner is visible on the website and is clearly the one running the show
- Business is independently owned (not a franchise)

**Scores 1 point if:** No visible evidence of internal technical capability.

---

### Scoring Interpretation

| Score | Action                                                                       |
| ----- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 3/3   | Top priority. Run the full audit. Personalize heavily. Send Day 1 this week. |
| 2/3   | Qualified. Add to list. Run the audit before sending Day 1.                  |
| 1/3   | Borderline. Add to a "monitor" category. Do not send outreach yet.           |
| 0/3   | Disqualify. Remove from list.                                                |

## Disqualification Rules

Remove immediately from the list if any of these are true. Do not spend time on these regardless of pain signals.

- **Franchise location:** The local owner does not control tech or vendor decisions. Corporate does. You would need to close corporate, which is a completely different (and much harder) sale.
- **Large corporate chain:** More than 3 locations, or visible corporate parent on the website. Same reason as franchise.
- **Fewer than 10 Google reviews:** Too early-stage. They likely don't have enough lead volume for automation to produce a measurable result, and they may not have the monthly budget.
- **Already has obvious automation:** Live chat widget on their website, instant text-back after a form submission, booking software actively in use. They have either solved the problem already or have someone helping them. You'd be competing against an existing solution rather than filling a gap.
- **Medical practice with complex HIPAA requirements:** Dentists and orthodontists are fine — they are on the target list. But hospitals, mental health practices, or any business that handles protected health records at scale has compliance requirements that are outside the current service scope.

## Notion Database Structure

Create one Notion database for all prospects. Every business gets one row. Update the row every time you take an action.

### Column Structure

| Column Name         | Type     | Notes                                                                                                |
| ------------------- | -------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Business Name       | Title    | Exact name as it appears on Google                                                                   |
| Owner Name          | Text     | First and last name — required before sending Day 1                                                  |
| Email               | Email    | Business or personal email                                                                           |
| Phone               | Phone    | Primary business number                                                                              |
| Website             | URL      | Direct link to their website                                                                         |
| Business Type       | Select   | Plumber / HVAC / Roofer / Dentist / Realtor / Cleaner                                                |
| City                | Text     | City or neighborhood                                                                                 |
| Review Count        | Number   | Total reviews on Google at time of research                                                          |
| Average Star Rating | Number   | Google star rating at time of research                                                               |
| Pain Signal Found   | Checkbox | Check if you found a specific, citable pain signal                                                   |
| Pain Signal Detail  | Text     | What you found — paste the review quote or describe the gap                                          |
| Qualification Score | Select   | 1 / 2 / 3                                                                                            |
| Audit Completed     | Checkbox | Check when 30-minute audit is done                                                                   |
| Outreach Status     | Select   | Not Started / Day 1 Sent / Day 3 Sent / Day 7 Sent / Responded / Booked / No Response / Disqualified |
| Day 1 Sent Date     | Date     | Exact date Day 1 was sent                                                                            |
| Day 3 Sent Date     | Date     | Set this when you send Day 1 — 3 days out                                                            |
| Day 7 Sent Date     | Date     | Set this when you send Day 1 — 7 days out                                                            |
| Loom Link           | URL      | Paste the Loom link once recorded                                                                    |
| Last Reply          | Text     | Brief note on what they said if they responded                                                       |
| Notes               | Text     | Anything else worth remembering about this prospect                                                  |

### Views to Create in Notion

**Active Sequence View:** Filter by Outreach Status = "Day 1 Sent" or "Day 3 Sent." Sort by Day 1 Sent Date ascending. This shows who needs a follow-up today.

**Top Prospects View:** Filter by Qualification Score = 3. Sort by Pain Signal Found (checked first). This is the shortlist for this week's outreach.

**Booked View:** Filter by Outreach Status = "Booked." This is your conversion rate proof.

**Full Pipeline View:** All rows, sorted by Day 1 Sent Date descending. Use this for your weekly review.

## Google Maps Research Methodology

**Step 1: Initial search**

Open Google Maps. Search "[business type] [city]" — for example, "plumber Austin" or "HVAC contractor Charlotte."

Click "View all" in the business panel on the left to see the full list. Google typically shows 20 results per page. Click through 3-4 pages to capture 60-80 raw prospects before filtering.

**Step 2: First-pass filter**

For each listing in the search results, quickly check:

- Review count: Skip anything under 10 reviews
- Does it look like an active business? (Has a website link, recent reviews)
- Is it a franchise or chain? (Check the name — "Roto-Rooter" is a franchise, "Johnson Plumbing" is likely independent)

Build a raw list of 30-40 that pass this quick filter.

**Step 3: Individual research (10-15 min each)**

For each surviving prospect:

1. Open their Google Business Profile — read the reviews, check the date of the most recent review, look for any review that mentions response time or communication
2. Open their website — check if it loads on mobile, count the contact methods, find the contact form if there is one
3. Find the owner's name — check the website About page first, then Google Business Profile, then LinkedIn
4. Run the qualification score (3 criteria above)
5. Add to Notion with all fields completed

**Step 4: Find contact information**

- Email: Check the website contact page. If not listed, look for a linked LinkedIn profile (many small business owners have their email on LinkedIn). As a last resort, use Hunter.io (free tier: 25 searches per month) to find email addresses by domain.
- Phone: Usually on the Google Business Profile or website header.

## Yelp Supplemental Research Methodology

Yelp surfaces businesses that don't rank as highly on Google Maps but still have real review volume. Use it after completing the Google Maps research for a given business type.

**Step 1:** Go to yelp.com. Search "[business type] [city]." Filter by: "16+ reviews" under the filter menu (Yelp equivalent of Google's 10+ minimum).

**Step 2:** For each result, check if they're already on your Notion list. Skip duplicates.

**Step 3:** For new businesses found on Yelp, run the same individual research process above. Note any Yelp reviews that mention communication or response time — these are especially useful for personalizing Day 1 outreach because prospects are less likely to expect you found them on Yelp.

**Step 4:** Check if they have a Google Business Profile at all. Businesses prominent on Yelp but weak on Google Maps may have a specific gap worth flagging in the audit.

## Weekly Prospecting Rhythm

Once outreach is running, maintain this cadence to keep the pipeline full without burning hours.

**Monday (30-60 minutes):** Research 5-10 new prospects. Add to Notion. Run qualification scoring. Identify top 3 for this week's outreach.

**Tuesday (30 minutes):** For the top 3 from Monday — run the 30-minute audit on each. Write personalized Day 1 emails. Send.

**Friday (45 minutes):** Check Notion for anyone who needs a Day 3 or Day 7 follow-up this week. Send follow-ups. Record any Day 7 Loom videos needed.

**Target output sustained over 4 weeks:** 20 prospects fully researched and in the sequence, 60 total outreach touchpoints, and 1-3 replies or booked calls.
