# Free Audit Framework

**The offer:** "I Found 3 Things Costing You Leads"

**What it is:** A personalized 30-minute research exercise specific to one business. The output is a 1-page written document and — for Day 7 prospects — a 3-minute Loom video walking through the findings on screen.

**Positioning:** Not a sales pitch. An observation. "I spent 20 minutes looking at your business from a customer's perspective and found these 3 things."

**When to run it:** Before sending Day 1 outreach to any prospect. The audit informs the personalized observation in the Day 1 email. The full output goes to Day 7.

---

## The 30-Minute Research Process

Work through these 5 areas in order. Take notes as you go. You're looking for gaps — places where a new customer tries to reach this business and fails, or where leads fall through without anyone catching them.

You will find 3 gaps to report. Most businesses have more than 3. Pick the 3 that are most concrete, most dollar-quantifiable, and most specific to this business's situation.

---

### Area 1: How Do They Handle After-Hours Contacts? (8 minutes)

**What to test:**

1. Call their business number at a time they're likely busy or closed. Note: does it ring and ring? Voicemail? What does the voicemail say? Does it invite them to leave a message, or is it the default carrier message? Is the voicemail full (a surprisingly common signal of a chaotic inbox)?

2. Submit their website contact form using a test name and email address. Start a timer. Note how long it takes to get any response — an auto-confirmation email, an actual reply, anything. If 24 hours pass with nothing: that's Gap 1.

3. Check if their Google Business Profile has messaging enabled (the "Message" button on Google Maps). If enabled, send a test message. Note the response time.

**What to look for:**

- No voicemail, or a voicemail that sounds inactive or full
- No auto-response email when someone submits their contact form
- No text-back within 5 minutes of a missed call
- No way to contact them after hours other than calling and hoping

**Gap description to use in the audit:**

"When someone contacts you after hours or during a busy period, there's no automatic response. They're sitting waiting to hear back. The competitor who gets back to them first — even with an automated text — wins the job."

---

### Area 2: What Does Their Google Business Profile Signal to New Leads? (5 minutes)

**What to check:**

1. Open Google Maps and find their listing. Note: review count, average star rating, date of their most recent review.

2. Read the last 10 reviews in full. Are any of them mentioning slow response, hard to reach, didn't call back, or similar communication complaints? Copy any relevant phrases — these are direct quotes you can use in the audit.

3. Is the profile complete? Check: hours listed and current, photos (5+ photos is the baseline), service list populated, Q&A section answered.

4. Have they responded to their reviews — especially negative ones? An unanswered negative review is a signal to every new customer who reads it.

**What to look for:**

- Reviews that explicitly mention slow response or difficulty reaching them (these are gold — cite them directly)
- Negative reviews with no owner response
- Last review was more than 60 days ago (suggests no active review request process)
- Missing or outdated hours
- Fewer than 20 reviews for a business that's been operating 3+ years

**Gap description to use in the audit:**

"Two of your recent reviews mention difficulty reaching you. For every person who writes that publicly, there are 5-10 who just left silently and went to a competitor. That's the quiet revenue leak that's hard to see in your numbers."

Or, if reviews are just slow: "Your review velocity has dropped off — you're averaging about one new review every [X] weeks. Businesses with a simple automated follow-up after each job get 3-5x more reviews, which directly impacts where you show up in Google search results."

---

### Area 3: What Does Their Website Do With a Visitor Who Doesn't Call? (7 minutes)

**What to check:**

1. Open their website on your phone, not your computer. Note the load time. Does it display properly on mobile? (More than half of local service business searches happen on phones.)

2. Find the contact options. Is "call us" the only way to reach them? Is there a contact form? Where is it on the page — prominent or buried?

3. If there's a contact form, count the fields. More than 5 fields is a conversion killer for service businesses. Note how many fields it has.

4. Submit the form (use a test name and email). What happens immediately after? Is there a thank-you page? A confirmation email? Or does the page just reset with no acknowledgment?

5. Is there any live chat, chatbot, or AI assistant on the site?

**What to look for:**

- Website that is unusable or hard to navigate on a phone
- "Call us" as the only contact method — no alternative for someone browsing at 9 PM who doesn't want to call
- Contact form with 8+ fields (name, address, phone, email, service type, availability, message, how did you hear about us...)
- No confirmation after form submission — leaves the visitor wondering if it went through
- No chat option, no booking widget, no way to take a next step without picking up the phone

**Gap description to use in the audit:**

"Your website has one contact method — a phone call. A growing segment of people, especially those under 40, won't call. They'll fill out a form, send a message, or just move to the next result. Right now, those people have no path to reach you. They leave."

Or if the form is too long: "Your contact form has [X] fields. Every additional field past the first 3 reduces submissions by roughly 10-15%. A name, phone number, and 'what do you need?' is all you need to capture a lead."

---

### Area 4: How Are They Handling Existing Customer Relationships? (5 minutes)

**What to check:**

1. Search for the business on Yelp and Facebook. Do they have reviews there? Are they responded to? A business that has no presence on Yelp and Facebook isn't necessarily bad — it's just one more signal that they're likely running everything manually.

2. Look at their Facebook or Instagram page. When was the last post? Do they respond to comments? Stale social media usually means: no systems, everything is done manually when there's time, which means rarely.

3. Look at their Google review velocity over time. Are reviews coming in steadily (suggests active review requests) or sporadically (suggests no system)? If their last review was more than 2 months ago, they almost certainly have no automated follow-up process.

**What to look for:**

- No recent reviews on any platform — the business exists but isn't actively collecting feedback
- No evidence of any post-job follow-up process (review requests, rebooking offers, check-ins)
- Stale social media or no social presence at all

**Gap description to use in the audit:**

"There's no visible system for following up after a job. For a business like yours, that means: no automatic review requests, no reminders to rebook, no check-in to make sure the customer was happy. Every job that ends without a follow-up is a potential review, referral, or repeat customer that slips away."

---

### Area 5: Speed-to-Lead Test (5 minutes)

**What to do:**

Submit an inquiry through whatever their primary contact channel is. This could be:

- The contact form on their website
- A Google Maps message (if enabled)
- A Facebook message
- A request through their booking tool (if they have one)

Use a test name. Start a timer the moment you submit.

Record exactly what happens:

- Did you get any immediate response (auto-email, auto-text, auto-reply)?
- How long until a real person responded?
- Did anyone respond at all?

**The benchmark:**

Leads contacted within 5 minutes are 100x more likely to convert than those contacted after 30 minutes. (Harvard Business Review / InsideSales.com research.) This is the fact you will cite in the audit and in your Day 3 follow-up email.

**What the timer tells you:**

- Response in under 5 minutes: They may already have something in place. Look for other gaps.
- Response in 5-60 minutes: Opportunity — they're trying but it's manual.
- Response in 1-24 hours: Clear gap. Use this as a primary finding.
- No response after 24 hours: This is Gap 1. Full stop. This is where money is being left on the table.

**Gap description to use in the audit:**

"I submitted a [form / message] on [date] at [time]. As of [date/time], [no response / response came after X hours]. Businesses that respond within 5 minutes are 100x more likely to close that lead. Every hour that passes, the odds drop."

## The Audit Output Document

After completing the research, write this 1-page document. Use the exact structure below. Customize every bracket. Nothing generic.

```
FREE LEAD AUDIT: [Business Name]
Completed by Sameer | [Date]

I spent 20 minutes reviewing [Business Name] from a new customer's perspective.
Here's what I found.

---

GAP 1: [Name of the gap — e.g., "No after-hours response system"]

What I found:
[Specific observation — what you actually tested or saw. One to two sentences, specific.]

Example: "I submitted your contact form on Tuesday at 7:40 PM. As of Wednesday at 9 AM — over 13 hours later — I hadn't received a confirmation email or any response."

What it's costing you:
[Estimate in leads or dollars. Keep it conservative — it's better to understate than to lose credibility.]

Example: "If 3 people per week submit after-hours and get no response, and half of those would have booked at your average job value of $600, that's approximately $3,600 in lost work every month."

What a fix looks like:
[One sentence. No jargon. No tools mentioned by name. Just the outcome.]

Example: "An automated response goes out within 60 seconds of any contact — text, email, or form — with a booking link and a message that tells them you'll follow up shortly."

---

GAP 2: [Name of the gap]

What I found:
[Specific observation]

What it's costing you:
[Estimate]

What a fix looks like:
[One sentence]

---

GAP 3: [Name of the gap]

What I found:
[Specific observation]

What it's costing you:
[Estimate]

What a fix looks like:
[One sentence]

---

Combined estimate:
These 3 gaps are likely costing [Business Name] approximately [X] leads per month. At an average job value of [$X], that's roughly [$X/month] in missed revenue.

---

Want to talk through what fixing these would look like for your business?

30 minutes, no obligation: [Cal.com booking link]
```

## Loom Video Walkthrough Format

The Loom is the same content as the written audit — but on screen, in your voice, showing their actual business. It converts better than text because it's personal and visual.

**Length:** 3 minutes exactly. Do not go longer.

**Structure:**

- 0:00-0:20 — Open with their name: "Hey [First Name], this is Sameer. I spent 20 minutes looking at [Business Name] from a customer's point of view. I found 3 things worth flagging. This will take 3 minutes."

- 0:20-1:10 — Gap 1 on screen: Pull up the actual page, form, or review. Show it. Narrate what you're seeing and what a customer experiences.

- 1:10-2:00 — Gap 2 on screen: Same approach. Pull up Google Maps, their profile, a review. Read it aloud if it's a review about response time.

- 2:00-2:40 — Gap 3 on screen: Show the evidence. Keep narration tight.

- 2:40-3:00 — Close with CTA: "These 3 things together are probably costing you somewhere between [low] and [high] in missed leads per month. I'm happy to walk through exactly what I'd do to fix each one — 30 minutes, no obligation. Link is in the email."

**Recording setup:** Loom Chrome extension or desktop app, Screen + Camera mode (face appears in the corner). This makes it feel personal rather than like a broadcast recording.

**Before sending:** Watch the recording back once. Check: Did you stay under 3 minutes? Is the screen legible? Does it feel like it was made for THIS business specifically?

---

## Quality Control — Before Every Audit

Run through this before you finalize any audit output:

- [ ] Every finding is specific to this business — no finding could apply to any other business without editing
- [ ] Every dollar estimate is conservative and clearly labeled as an estimate
- [ ] No technical jargon appears anywhere (no "automation," "workflow," "API," "webhook," "AI")
- [ ] The "what a fix looks like" section describes an outcome, not a technology
- [ ] The combined estimate uses their actual average job value, not a generic number
- [ ] The Cal.com link is correct and goes to a live booking page
